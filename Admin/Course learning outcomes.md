# Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs)

**GPSE 100: Positive Psychology for Academic and Career Success (Advancement Semester, B2)**

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

---

## Core Content (Positive Psychology)

1. Explain and apply major theoretical frameworks in positive psychology (e.g., PERMA, broaden-and-build theory, self-determination theory) to analyze human flourishing across individual, social, and cultural contexts.  

2. Critically evaluate empirical research and claims in positive psychology using a course-provided evaluation framework. This includes identifying methodological strengths and limitations, distinguishing correlation from causation, and recognizing potential biases and ethical issues.  

---

## Language and Communication

3. Comprehend, summarize, and synthesize CEFR B2-level academic texts in psychology (e.g., articles and book chapters). Apply reading strategies to evaluate source relevance and quality, and integrate evidence using appropriate citation practices.  

4. Use disciplinary terminology and academic vocabulary from psychology and related social sciences accurately and effectively in both spoken and written communication.  

5. Participate effectively in academic discussions and brief structured presentations about well-being, responding to peers’ perspectives and supporting claims with evidence.  

---

## Applied and Integrated Learning

6. Design and conduct a small inquiry using AI tools (e.g., a large language model) and/or basic well-being data to explore a positive psychology question. This includes formulating a research question, handling data ethically, conducting basic analysis, and reporting findings.  

7. Engage in evidence-based well-being practices (e.g., gratitude, strengths identification, goal-setting, and self-regulation), and reflect critically on their application to academic work, career planning, and everyday contexts, drawing on research evidence.  

8. Develop and communicate (orally and in writing) an evidence-informed intervention plan for a real student-life challenge (e.g., stress in group work, social isolation, language barriers), integrating research evidence and considering relevant social and cultural factors.
