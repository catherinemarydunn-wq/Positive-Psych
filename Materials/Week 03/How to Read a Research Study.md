# How to Read a Research Study  
*GPSE 100 – Week 3*

## ⚠️ Note to Students
This guide will help you read and understand research articles in this course. You are **not expected to understand everything**. Instead, focus on identifying key information.

---

## Why Are We Reading Research?

In this course, research helps us:
- understand how psychological ideas are tested  
- evaluate whether claims are supported by evidence  
- develop critical thinking skills  

👉 Your goal is not to become a researcher.  
👉 Your goal is to **understand the basic structure of a study**.

---

## The 4 Key Parts of a Research Study

Most research articles follow a similar structure. Focus on these four parts:

---

### 1. Purpose (Research Question)

This tells you:
👉 What is the study trying to find out?

Look for phrases like:
- “The purpose of this study is…”  
- “This study examines…”  

**Example:**  
Does practicing gratitude improve well-being?

---

### 2. Method (What Did They Do?)

This explains:
👉 How the study was conducted

Focus on:
- Who participated?  
- What did they do?  
- Were there different groups?

**Key term:**
- **Random assignment** = participants are placed into groups by chance  
👉 This makes the study more reliable

---

### 3. Results (What Did They Find?)

This tells you:
👉 What happened in the study

Look for:
- patterns  
- differences between groups  
- general findings  

👉 You do **not** need to understand statistics.  
Focus on the main outcomes.

---

### 4. Conclusion (What Does It Mean?)

This explains:
👉 How the researchers interpret the results

Ask yourself:
- What do the researchers think the results show?  
- Do they make strong or cautious claims?

---

## Important Concept: Correlation vs. Causation

This is one of the most important ideas in this course.

### Correlation
Two things are related.

**Example:**  
Students who sleep more have better grades.

👉 They are connected, but one may not cause the other.

---

### Causation
One thing directly causes another.

**Example:**  
A new study method improves test scores.

👉 This usually requires a **controlled experiment**.

---

### Why This Matters

Even in experiments:
- results may not be perfect  
- other factors may influence outcomes  

👉 Always ask:
**Does this study show correlation or causation?**

---

## How to Read Efficiently

You do not need to read every word.

Follow this strategy:

1. Read the **Purpose**  
2. Skim the **Method**  
3. Focus on the **Results**  
4. Read the **Conclusion carefully**

👉 This saves time and improves understanding.

---

## Common Challenges (and What to Do)

### ❗ “There are too many difficult words”
→ Focus on meaning, not every word

### ❗ “I don’t understand the statistics”
→ Ignore them and look for the main idea

### ❗ “I feel lost”
→ Go back to the 4 questions:
- What is the question?  
- What did they do?  
- What did they find?  
- What does it mean?  

---

## Your Task When Reading

When you read a study, you should be able to answer:

1. What is the research question?  
2. What did the researchers do?  
3. What did they find?  
4. Does this show correlation or causation? Why?  

---

## Key Takeaway

You do not need to understand everything in a research article.

👉 If you can identify:
- the question  
- the method  
- the results  

then you are successfully reading research.
