# Understanding Simulation and Models in Research  
*(Instructional Guide for Student Inquiry Projects)*

---

## ⚠️ Note to Students

This guide explains how simulation is used in your inquiry project. The goal is to help you understand how your study works and what your results mean. You are not expected to understand programming or advanced statistics.

---

## Introduction

In many research studies, data is collected from real people. However, in this course, you will use a different approach. Instead of collecting real data, you will create a *simulation*.

A simulation is a model of a real-world situation. It allows researchers to explore what might happen under certain conditions. In your project, you will design a study, and then a computer system will generate data based on your design.

This approach allows you to learn the full research process without needing to recruit participants or collect real data.

---

## What Is a Model?

A model is a simplified representation of reality. It includes only the most important elements needed to answer a specific question.

For example, if you are studying gratitude and well-being, your model might include:

- whether a person practices gratitude  
- their level of well-being  
- how strongly these two variables are connected  

The model does not include everything about a person’s life. Instead, it focuses on the key factors that are relevant to your research question.

---

## What Is Simulation?

Simulation is the process of using a model to generate data.

In your project, you will:

1. Design a research question  
2. Decide what variables to include  
3. Make assumptions about how those variables are related  
4. Use a system to generate data based on those assumptions  

For example, you might assume that gratitude practice increases well-being. The simulation will then generate responses that reflect this assumption.

---

## The Role of Assumptions

Assumptions are a central part of simulation.

When you design your study, you decide:

- which variables matter  
- how strongly they are related  
- what patterns you expect to see  

These decisions are not neutral. They reflect your understanding of the topic, based on course readings and theory.

For example, if you assume that gratitude has a strong positive effect, your simulated data will show a strong relationship. If you assume a weak effect, the pattern will be smaller.

Because of this, the results of your simulation depend on the assumptions you make.

---

## What Your Results Mean

It is important to understand what your results represent.

The data you generate does not come from real people. It comes from your model.

This means that your results do not show how people actually behave. Instead, they show what would happen *if your assumptions are correct*.

A useful way to think about this is:

> “Given these assumptions, the simulation produced these results.”

Your findings suggest possibilities. They do not prove anything about the real world.

---

## Why Use Simulation?

Simulation has several advantages for learning.

First, it allows you to practice the full research process, from question design to data analysis.

Second, it makes your assumptions visible. In many real studies, assumptions are hidden. In simulation, you must state them clearly.

Third, it allows you to test ideas quickly. You can change your assumptions and see how the results change.

This helps you understand how research design affects outcomes.

---

## Limitations of Simulation

Simulation also has important limitations.

Because the data is generated by your model, it cannot surprise you in the same way that real data can. It reflects the logic of your design, not the complexity of real human behavior.

In addition, your model may be incomplete. You may leave out important variables or oversimplify relationships.

Finally, if your assumptions are unrealistic, your results will also be unrealistic.

For these reasons, it is important to be honest about the limits of your study.

---

## Conclusion

Simulation is a powerful tool for learning about research. It allows you to design a study, test your assumptions, and interpret results in a structured way.

At the same time, it is important to remember that simulation is not the same as studying real people. Your results show what is possible under certain conditions, not what is true in the real world.

Understanding this distinction is a key part of thinking critically about research.
