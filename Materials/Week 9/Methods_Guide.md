# Designing a Simple Research Study  
*(Instructional Guide for Student Inquiry Projects)*

---

## ⚠️ Note to Students

This guide is designed to help you plan your own small research project. It simplifies key ideas from research methods to support learning and application. You are not expected to understand advanced statistics or complex research designs.

---

## Introduction

Research is a way of asking and answering questions in a systematic and organized way. In this course, you will design a small inquiry project to explore a topic related to well-being.

This guide will help you understand how to develop a research question, identify variables, and design a simple study.

---

## Research Questions

A research study begins with a clear question. A good research question is focused, specific, and possible to answer with the time and resources available.

For example, instead of asking “What is happiness?”, a more effective question might be:  
“How does gratitude journaling affect students’ mood over one week?”

A strong research question usually focuses on a specific group, examines a relationship or effect, and can be explored using data.

---

## Variables

In research, a *variable* is something that can change or vary.

For example, in the question above, gratitude journaling is one variable and mood is another variable.

Researchers are often interested in how one variable influences another. The variable that is changed or controlled is called the *independent variable*, and the variable that is measured is called the *dependent variable*.

Understanding variables is important because they help you define what you are studying and how you will measure it.

---

## Types of Data

Researchers collect different types of data to answer their questions.

One common type is *quantitative data*, which includes numbers. For example, students might rate their mood on a scale from 1 to 10.

Another type is *qualitative data*, which includes words or descriptions. For example, students might describe how they felt after completing an activity.

Both types of data can provide useful information, and many studies use a combination of both.

---

## Study Design

A study design is a plan for how you will collect and analyze your data. In this course, you will design a simple and manageable study.

One common approach is a survey-based study, where participants answer a set of questions. Surveys are useful because they are easy to distribute and can collect data from multiple people.

Another approach is a simple intervention study, where participants complete an activity (such as gratitude journaling) and report their experiences.

When designing your study, it is important to think about who your participants will be, what they will do, and how you will measure the results.

---

## Sampling

A *sample* refers to the group of people who participate in your study. In most cases, it is not possible to study everyone, so researchers select a smaller group.

In this course, your sample will likely include classmates or people you know. While this type of sample is convenient, it is important to recognize that it may not represent all populations.

---

## Ethics in Research

Ethics are an important part of any research study. Researchers must ensure that participants are treated fairly and respectfully.

In your project, you should explain the purpose of the study, allow participants to choose whether to participate, protect participants’ privacy, and avoid collecting sensitive or harmful information.

Participants should feel safe and comfortable throughout the study.

---

## Data Analysis

After collecting data, researchers look for patterns or trends.

For example, you might calculate average scores, look at changes over time, or identify common themes in responses.

You do not need to use advanced statistics. The goal is to identify meaningful patterns that help answer your research question.

---

## Limitations

All studies have limitations. A limitation is something that may affect the accuracy or generalizability of your results.

Common limitations include small sample size, short time period, and lack of control over variables.

Recognizing limitations is an important part of academic honesty and critical thinking.

---

## Conclusion

Designing a research study involves asking a clear question, collecting relevant data, and analyzing the results thoughtfully. Even a small study can provide valuable insights when it is carefully planned.

In this course, your goal is not to conduct perfect research, but to engage with the process and develop your understanding of how research works.
