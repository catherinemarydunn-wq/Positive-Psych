# Interpreting Results in Research  
*(Instructional Guide for Student Inquiry Projects)*

---

## ⚠️ Note to Students

This guide will help you understand how to interpret and explain the results of your inquiry project. The goal is not to produce perfect results, but to explain your findings clearly and thoughtfully.

---

## Introduction

After completing a study, the next step is to interpret the results. This means explaining what your data shows and what it might mean.

In this course, your results come from a simulation. This means that your data reflects the assumptions you made in your model, not the behavior of real people. Because of this, it is especially important to interpret your results carefully.

---

## Describing Your Results

The first step in interpretation is to describe what you observe in your data.

You might notice patterns such as:

- one group having higher scores than another  
- a change in scores over time  
- a relationship between two variables  

When describing results, focus on what the data shows, not what you think it means yet.

For example, instead of writing “gratitude improves well-being,” you could write:

> “Participants in the gratitude group had higher well-being scores than those in the control group.”

This keeps your description accurate and neutral.

---

## Explaining Your Results

After describing the results, you can begin to explain them.

This involves connecting your findings to your research question and your assumptions. For example, if you assumed that gratitude would increase well-being, your results may support that assumption.

However, it is important to use careful language. Instead of making strong claims, use words that show uncertainty, such as:

- suggests  
- may indicate  
- is consistent with  
- appears to  

For example:

> “These results suggest that gratitude practice may be associated with higher well-being.”

This type of language reflects the fact that your findings are not final proof.

---

## Understanding Uncertainty

All research involves uncertainty. This means that results are not exact and may change with different data or conditions.

In your project, uncertainty can come from:

- the assumptions you made  
- the size of your sample  
- the way your variables were defined  

When interpreting your results, it is important to acknowledge this uncertainty.

For example:

> “While the results suggest a positive relationship, there is still uncertainty about the strength of this effect.”

Recognizing uncertainty is a sign of strong academic thinking.

---

## Avoiding Overgeneralization

A common mistake in research is making claims that are too strong.

In this course, it is especially important to avoid statements such as:

- “This proves that…”  
- “All people…”  
- “This always happens…”  

Your results do not apply to all people or all situations. They apply only to your specific model and assumptions.

A better approach is to say:

> “These findings suggest a possible pattern that could be explored further.”

---

## Connecting Results to Assumptions

Because your study is based on simulation, your results are closely connected to your assumptions.

If your model assumes a strong effect, your results will likely show a strong pattern. If your assumptions change, your results may also change.

For this reason, it is important to reflect on how your design influenced your findings.

For example:

> “The strength of this relationship may be influenced by the assumptions built into the model.”

This shows that you understand how your study works.

---

## Discussing Limitations

A strong interpretation includes a discussion of limitations.

Limitations are factors that may affect the accuracy or meaning of your results.

In your project, common limitations include:

- the use of simulated data  
- simplified models of human behavior  
- limited variables  

For example:

> “Because the data was generated through simulation, the results reflect the assumptions of the model rather than real-world behavior.”

Including limitations shows critical thinking and honesty.

---

## Conclusion

Interpreting results involves more than simply reporting findings. It requires careful thinking, clear language, and an understanding of uncertainty.

In this course, your goal is to explain what your results show, how they relate to your assumptions, and what they might suggest for future research.

Strong interpretation does not mean being certain. It means being thoughtful, precise, and aware of the limits of your study.
