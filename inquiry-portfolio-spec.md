# LLM Interaction Portfolio

## Purpose

Students curate a selection of their LLM conversation logs from the inquiry workflow and submit them as evidence of their language use during the project. The portfolio is individually submitted: each student selects from their own interactions.

This parallels the portfolio model in GEAP 103 (Basic Computer Skills), where students documented their LLM interactions as part of ongoing assessment. Students who completed GEAP 103 will be familiar with the format.

## What to include

Select 6–8 exchanges from across the inquiry workflow that show:

1. **A design specification** — an exchange where the student specified a design choice (participant characteristics, instrument, effect model, priors) and the LLM executed it. The exchange should show the specification, the LLM's output, and the student's evaluation.

2. **An evaluation or revision** — an exchange where the student identified a problem in LLM output (a double-barrelled survey item, a code summary that didn't match the spec, a prior predictive plot that produced implausible predictions) and iterated to fix it.

3. **A justification from readings** — an exchange where the student used course literature to justify an effect size, a prior, or a design choice. The exchange should show the student engaging with the source, not just asking the LLM for a number.

4. **An interpretation** — an exchange where the student interpreted analysis output (a posterior, a credible interval, a group-means plot) and articulated what it meant for the research question.

Students may include additional exchanges that show strong work in any category. Not every exchange needs to be successful; an exchange where the student caught an error or changed their mind based on LLM feedback can be strong evidence of critical evaluation.

## Format

For each selected exchange:

1. **Label** — which category above (specification, evaluation, justification, interpretation) and which workflow step (e.g., "Step 3, instrument design").
2. **The exchange itself** — the student's prompt and the LLM's response. May be truncated if the full exchange is long, but the relevant portions must be complete.
3. **Annotation** (2–3 sentences) — the student explains why they selected this exchange and what it shows about their work.

The portfolio can be submitted as a document, a folder of conversation exports, or a curated set of links (if the LLM tool supports shareable conversations).

## Scope

6–8 exchanges is the target. More is acceptable if genuinely strong; padding with weak examples dilutes the portfolio. The annotations should total roughly 500–800 words across all entries.

## Assessment criteria

The portfolio is assessed on:

- **Range** — does the selection cover different stages of the workflow and different types of language use (specifying, evaluating, justifying, interpreting)?
- **Quality of the student's contributions** — are the prompts clear and specific? Do the evaluations identify real problems? Do the justifications engage with sources? Do the interpretations go beyond restating the LLM's output?
- **Quality of the annotations** — do they accurately describe what the exchange shows? Are they written in appropriate academic register?
- **Evidence of iteration** — does the portfolio show the student refining their work based on LLM feedback, not just accepting first-pass output?

## Relationship to the group project

The inquiry is a group project, but the portfolio is individual. Each student's LLM interactions are their own. The portfolio provides individual evidence of contribution and language use that the group report and presentation cannot.

## Notes for instructors

- Students should be told at the start of the inquiry to save their LLM conversation logs. Curation happens at the end, but the raw material must be preserved throughout.
- The portfolio complements, but does not replace, other individual accountability mechanisms (oral defence, individual reflection, peer evaluation). The instructor decides which combination to use.
- Assessment should focus on the quality of the student's language and reasoning in the exchanges, not on the quality of the LLM's output.
