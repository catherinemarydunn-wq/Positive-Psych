# Mini Inquiry Reading Test

## Purpose

This test assesses the reading skills that persist regardless of LLM access: verifying claims against sources, catching errors and omissions, and evaluating whether conclusions follow from evidence. These are the same skills students exercise when they check an LLM's summary of a paper, evaluate whether an LLM-generated effect size is defensible, or assess whether a claim in a report is supported by the data.

The test is individually administered without LLM access. It is not a test of reading without technology; it is a test of the reading skills that technology does not replace.

## Construct

The test measures source verification reading: the ability to compare a derived claim to its source and evaluate accuracy, completeness, and inferential validity.

This is distinct from the course-level reading comprehension assessment (which tests comprehension of psychology texts). The inquiry reading test specifically targets the verification and error-detection skills the inquiry workflow demands.

## Format

Students receive a short source text (300–500 words, excerpted from a positive psychology reading at B2 level) and one or more derived claims based on that source. The claims may be accurate, partially accurate, misleading, or wrong. The student evaluates each claim against the source.

**Time:** 30–40 minutes.
**Materials:** Source text + derived claims (printed or on screen). No LLM access, no notes.

## Item types

### Type 1: Accurate or inaccurate?

A claim is presented alongside the source. The student judges whether the claim accurately represents the source and explains why or why not.

**Example:**

> **Source excerpt** (adapted from Emmons & McCullough, 2003): "Participants in the gratitude condition reported fewer physical symptoms (e.g., headaches, chest pain) and more time spent exercising than participants in the hassles condition. There was no significant difference between conditions on measures of positive or negative affect at the daily level, though the gratitude group did report higher life satisfaction at the end of the study period."
>
> **Claim:** "Emmons and McCullough (2003) found that practising gratitude significantly increases positive emotions and life satisfaction."
>
> **What the student must do:** Identify that the claim overstates the findings. The source reports no significant difference in positive affect (contradicting "increases positive emotions") and reports higher life satisfaction only at the study-period level, not as a daily effect. The claim is partially accurate (life satisfaction) but misleading (positive emotions).

### Type 2: What is missing?

A summary of the source is presented. The student identifies what the summary omits that a reader would need to know.

**Example:**

> **Source excerpt** (adapted from Crede, Tynan, & Harms, 2017): "Our meta-analysis of grit research found that grit's predictive validity for academic performance (rho = .18) was modest and largely attributable to the perseverance-of-effort facet rather than consistency-of-interest. After controlling for conscientiousness, grit's incremental validity was near zero. The original Duckworth et al. (2007) studies reported substantially larger effects, but these were based on highly restricted samples (West Point cadets, spelling bee finalists)."
>
> **Summary:** "A meta-analysis by Crede et al. (2017) found that grit predicts academic performance with a correlation of .18."
>
> **What the student must do:** Identify that the summary omits (a) the finding that the effect is driven by one facet only, (b) that grit adds nothing beyond conscientiousness, and (c) that the original studies used atypical samples. The summary is technically accurate but misleading by omission.

### Type 3: Does the conclusion follow?

A source excerpt is presented alongside a conclusion someone drew from it. The student evaluates whether the conclusion is warranted.

**Example:**

> **Source excerpt** (adapted from Killingsworth, 2021): "Experienced well-being rose roughly linearly with log(income) across the full income range studied, with no evidence of a plateau at $75,000. This contradicts the satiation hypothesis proposed by Kahneman and Deaton (2010). However, the measure of experienced well-being used here (momentary self-reports via smartphone) differs from Kahneman and Deaton's measure (retrospective daily reports of affect)."
>
> **Conclusion:** "Killingsworth (2021) proved that Kahneman and Deaton were wrong about the $75,000 threshold."
>
> **What the student must do:** Identify that the conclusion overstates. Killingsworth's finding contradicts the satiation hypothesis using a different measure. The source itself notes the measurement difference, which means the two studies may not be directly comparable. "Proved wrong" is too strong; "found conflicting evidence using a different method" is more accurate.

### Type 4: Effect-size translation

A source reports an effect in one metric. A claim translates it to the student's instrument scale. The student evaluates whether the translation is defensible.

**Example:**

> **Source excerpt** (adapted from a meta-analysis): "The average effect of gratitude interventions on subjective well-being was d = 0.31 (95% CI [0.18, 0.44]) across 38 studies. Most studies used multi-item well-being scales (SWLS, PANAS) with varying numbers of items and response formats."
>
> **Claim:** "Based on this meta-analysis, I set my simulation's gratitude effect to +0.8 on a 1–7 Likert scale."
>
> **What the student must do:** Evaluate whether d = 0.31 maps to 0.8 points on a 7-point scale. If the SD of the outcome is approximately 1.0–1.2 (typical for 7-point Likert), then d = 0.31 corresponds to roughly 0.3–0.4 points, not 0.8. The student should identify that the claimed effect is approximately double the meta-analytic estimate and note this as an overstatement.

## Scoring

Each item is scored on a 3-point scale:

- **2:** Identifies the core issue accurately and explains it with reference to specific details in the source.
- **1:** Identifies that something is wrong but the explanation is vague, incomplete, or mislocates the problem.
- **0:** Fails to identify the issue, or identifies a non-issue.

## Notes for instructors

- Source excerpts should be adapted from actual course readings or closely related texts at B2 level. Do not use sources students have not encountered in class — the test assesses source verification, not cold reading of unfamiliar material.
- The derived claims should resemble the kinds of errors LLMs actually produce: overstatement, omission of qualifications, conflation of different measures, overgeneralization from restricted samples.
- 4–6 items is sufficient for a 30–40 minute test.
- The test can be administered as part of the pre-registration checkpoint (before the group commits their design spec) or as a standalone component at any point during the inquiry.
